# 📖 Project Overview
This project analyzes intake and outcome data from the City of Long Beach Animal Care Services; a real-world shelter dataset covering over 52,000 animals across multiple species, intake types, conditions, and outcome categories.

The goal is to uncover operational patterns, identify what drives successful placements, and surface where strategic effort will have the greatest impact on animal welfare and shelter efficiency. The final deliverable is a four-page interactive Power BI dashboard built for shelter managers, community partners, and city stakeholders.

---

## 🎯 Why This Project Matters
Every day an animal spends in a shelter costs money, space, and stress. For the shelter, every animal that leaves alive is an operational win.
This dashboard was built for one purpose; to give shelter operators the visibility to act faster and smarter:

🐕 Which animals are at risk of long stays before it becomes a crisis?
📍 Which neighborhoods need the most outreach to reduce stray intake?
📉 Why did the Live Release Rate drop and what brings it back up?

Because in animal welfare, data isn't just reporting. It's the difference between a life saved and a life lost.

## Business questions
1. How do intake and outcome volumes change over time - by month, season, and day of week?
2. What is the shelter's Live Release Rate and how has it trended over the years?
3. Which species and age groups have higher or lower live release rates?
4. Which types of pets are adopted most often?
5. How has the number of adoptions changed over the years
6. What is the average length of stay, and how does it vary across animal profiles?
7. Which animals tend to stay the longest, and what do they have in common?
8. Do certain intake conditions lead to different outcomes?
9. Which intake sources contribute the largest volumes?
10. Are there animals returning to the shelter more than once?
11. Which neighborhoods have the lowest live release rates?
12. What factors best predict a long shelter stay?

## 💡 Key Insights
- Wednesday is the hardest day to manage. There are 10,337 animals intake on Wednesdays alone which is more than double Sunday (4,100).
- Summer overwhelms the shelter. Intake hits 16,500 in summer vs 8,500 in winter. Seasonal surge planning is not optional, it's operational survival.
- The Live Release Rate hit rock bottom in 2018 at 74% recovering to 81% by recent years, but still far from the 100% achieved in 2013–14. Something changed and understanding what drove both the drop and recovery is critical.
- Behavioral condition animals stay 54 days on average, nearly 3x the shelter average of 19 days. These animals are consuming disproportionate resources.
- Rabbits stay 46 days on average, more than double cats (24 days) and dogs (20 days), yet they receive far less adoption attention and marketing.
- Westminster's LRR is just 16.7% - catastrophically low compared to the shelter average of 79%. This single neighborhood is a significant drag on overall outcomes.
- Stray animals account for 70% of all intakes (36,780) and are also the most frequent repeaters (270 dogs returned as strays). The stray problem is both an intake and a repeat-cycle problem.
- 1–3 year old animals have the highest LRR at 86.2% while unknown age groups sit at 58.2%. Documenting age could improve placement outcomes.

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaways
- Operational strain is predictable. Summer surges and Wednesday spikes follow a clear pattern that should drive staffing decisions, not surprise them.
- The shelter's biggest length-of-stay problem isn't dogs or cats, it's rabbits and behaviorally complex animals that aren't getting targeted placement support.
- Westminster is an outlier that demands investigation. A 16.7% LRR is not a data anomaly, it's a systemic failure in one jurisdiction.
- Strays are a revolving door without community-level intervention. The same animals will cycle through the shelter repeatedly, consuming resources with no permanent outcome.
Age and condition documentation gaps directly hurt placement. Unknown age animals have a 28-point LRR gap versus well-documented animals.
Adoption is growing. From 1 adoption in 2013 to 2,073 in 2023, the upward trajectory is real and worth accelerating

---

## ✅ Strategic Recommendations
- Realign staffing to Wednesday and summer demand. Schedule peak staff on Wednesdays and begin surge planning in April ahead of summer intake spikes.
- Launch a targeted Rabbit adoption campaign. Rabbits stay nearly 3x longer than the average. Dedicated marketing, foster programs, and rescue partnerships can dramatically cut their LOS.
Investigate and intervene in Westminster urgently — a 16.7% LRR requires a dedicated audit of what outcomes are occurring there and why.
Expand community stray prevention programs; TNR (Trap-Neuter-Return), community microchipping drives, and owner education in Long Beach (45,113 intakes) can meaningfully reduce intake volume at the source.
Prioritize behavioral rehabilitation resources. Animals with behavioral conditions average 54 days. Early behavioral assessment and intervention can shorten stays and improve adoptability.
Standardize age documentation at intake. Closing the 28-point LRR gap between known and unknown age animals starts with a simple process fix at the intake desk.
